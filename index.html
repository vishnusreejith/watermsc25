Lab Analysis Guide

A Practical Guide to Environmental Sampling & Analysis

This guide provides a comprehensive overview of the standard procedures for collecting, preserving, and analyzing water and soil samples from contaminated environments. Accurate analysis begins with robust sampling, and this resource details the critical steps for obtaining reliable data.

Key Sections:

  • Wastewater Sampling: Techniques for collecting representative samples and ensuring their integrity through proper preservation.
  • Soil Sampling: Methods for sample collection from contaminated sites and subsequent laboratory processing.
  • Analytical Methods: Detailed principles and procedures for estimating key pollution indicators, from BOD and COD to heavy metals and pesticides.
  • Practical Experiments: Step-by-step examples of common environmental laboratory experiments.

Wastewater Sampling: Collection and Preservation

Sampling Methods

Grab Sampling

A single sample collected at a specific time and location. It represents the water quality at that precise moment.

Composite Sampling

A mixture of grab samples from the same point but at different times. This provides an average concentration over a period.

Sample Collection and Preservation

Preservation is crucial to prevent chemical and biological changes between collection and analysis. The table below outlines standard preservation protocols.

Soil Sampling from Contaminated Sites

Sample Collection Tools

The objective is to obtain a sample that represents the area of contamination. Common tools include soil augers, core samplers, and spades. Samples are typically collected from different depths to understand the vertical distribution of contaminants. For volatile organic compounds (VOCs), samples must be taken with minimal disturbance and sealed immediately.

Soil Auger

Used for boring into soil to collect disturbed samples.

Core Sampler

Extracts an intact, cylindrical core for undisturbed samples.

Sample Processing in the Laboratory

  1. Drying: Samples are typically air-dried or oven-dried at a low temperature (e.g., <40°C) to prevent loss of volatile compounds.
  2. Sieving: Dried soil is passed through a 2 mm sieve to remove stones, roots, and other large debris, ensuring a homogenous sample for analysis.
  3. Grinding: The sieved sample may be ground to a fine powder to ensure complete extraction of contaminants during analysis.
  4. Storage: Processed samples are stored in clean, labeled containers in a cool, dark place.

Laboratory Analysis Methods

Estimation of DO, BOD, and COD

Dissolved Oxygen (DO): Typically estimated using the Winkler method (an iodometric titration) or a DO meter. Low DO indicates pollution from oxygen-demanding wastes.

Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD): Measures the amount of oxygen consumed by microorganisms while decomposing organic matter. The standard test involves incubating a sealed water sample for 5 days at 20°C and measuring the change in DO. BOD₅ = (Initial DO - Final DO) x Dilution Factor.

Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD): Measures the oxygen equivalent of the organic matter susceptible to oxidation by a strong chemical oxidant (potassium dichromate). It's a faster method than BOD and measures both biodegradable and non-biodegradable organics.

Enumeration of Microorganisms

Total Coliforms in Wastewater: Coliforms are indicator organisms for fecal contamination. They are enumerated using the Most Probable Number (MPN) method or the Membrane Filtration (MF) technique. The MPN method is a statistical estimation based on the number of positive results in a series of dilutions, while the MF technique involves filtering water and counting colonies on a selective agar medium.

Microorganisms from Polluted Habitats: Techniques like serial dilution and plating on selective or differential media are used to isolate and quantify specific groups of bacteria (e.g., hydrocarbon degraders, sulfate reducers) from soil or water to assess the microbial community structure and bioremediation potential.

Estimation of Specific Contaminants

Organic Carbon (Soil/Sludge): Estimated using the Walkley-Black method, which involves wet oxidation of organic matter with a chromic acid mixture and titration of the unreacted oxidant.

Heavy Metals (Soil/Wastewater): After acid digestion to bring metals into solution, their concentration is determined using Atomic Absorption Spectrophotometry (AAS) or Inductively Coupled Plasma (ICP) Mass Spectrometry, which offer high sensitivity and specificity.

Pesticide Residue Analysis (Soil/Water): Involves solvent extraction of the residues, followed by a clean-up step to remove interferences. Final analysis is typically done using Gas Chromatography (GC) or High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC), often coupled with a mass spectrometer (MS) for definitive identification.

Practical Experiment Examples

Experiment 1: Determining BOD₅ of a Wastewater Sample

Objective: To measure the amount of dissolved oxygen consumed by microorganisms during the decomposition of organic matter in a water sample over a 5-day period.

Procedure:

1
Sample Preparation: If the sample is expected to have high BOD, prepare several dilutions using aerated, nutrient-rich dilution water. This ensures the final DO reading is within the detectable range.
2
Initial DO Measurement: Measure the initial dissolved oxygen (DO₁) of one set of sample dilutions immediately using the Winkler method or a DO meter.
3
Incubation: Seal a parallel set of dilution bottles, ensuring no air bubbles are trapped inside. Incubate these bottles in the dark at 20°C for 5 days.
4
Final DO Measurement: After 5 days, measure the final dissolved oxygen (DO₅) in the incubated bottles.
5
Calculation: Calculate the BOD₅ using the formula:
BOD₅ (mg/L) = (DO₁ - DO₅) x Dilution Factor

Experiment 2: Enumerating Total Coliforms by Membrane Filtration

Objective: To quantify the number of coliform bacteria in a water sample, which are indicators of fecal contamination.

Procedure:

1
Filtration: Pass a known volume of the water sample (e.g., 100 mL) through a sterile 0.45 µm pore size membrane filter. The bacteria are retained on the filter's surface.
2
Incubation: Aseptically place the membrane filter onto a petri dish containing a selective medium (like m-Endo agar), which allows coliforms to grow while inhibiting other bacteria. Incubate at 35°C for 22-24 hours.
3
Colony Counting: After incubation, count the colonies that exhibit a characteristic metallic green sheen. These are identified as coliforms.
4
Calculation: Report the results as Colony Forming Units (CFU) per 100 mL of the sample.
Coliforms/100 mL = (Number of colonies counted / Volume of sample filtered in mL) x 100

Experiment 3: Heavy Metal (e.g., Lead) Analysis in Soil

Objective: To determine the concentration of a specific heavy metal, such as lead (Pb), in a contaminated soil sample using Atomic Absorption Spectrophotometry (AAS).

Procedure Summary:

1
Acid Digestion: Breaking Down the Sample

The goal of digestion is to completely break down the complex soil matrix (organic matter, silicates) and release the target heavy metals into a liquid (aqueous) form. A precisely weighed, dried soil sample is placed in a digestion vessel with a mixture of strong acids, commonly nitric acid (HNO₃) and hydrochloric acid (HCl), known as aqua regia. Nitric acid is a powerful oxidizing agent that destroys organic matter, while hydrochloric acid helps dissolve mineral components. The mixture is then heated, either on a hot plate in a fume hood or in a sealed vessel within a microwave digestion system, to accelerate the chemical reactions. After cooling, the solution is filtered to remove any remaining particulate matter and diluted to a precise volume with deionized water. This final solution is now ready for analysis.

2
Analysis by AAS: The digested sample solution is introduced into the AAS instrument. The AAS measures the amount of light absorbed by the lead atoms at a specific wavelength, which is directly proportional to the concentration of lead in the sample.
3
Quantification: The concentration is determined by comparing the absorbance of the sample to a calibration curve created using standard solutions of known lead concentrations. The final result is reported in mg of lead per kg of dry soil (mg/kg).
